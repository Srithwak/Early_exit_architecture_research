a) A very clear and specific problem you want to solve (you’ve finalized the problem statement so 
far) 
●​ Energy Use in Neural Networks: Application for Seizure Detection 
Our research project targets the problem of high energy usage and computational cost in neural 
networks for seizure detection. Traditional deep neural networks process each data layer in full 
when oftentimes they can be classified in early on layers. This takes unnecessary time, energy, 
and memory which can lead to problems in real-world healthcare situations. Many neural 
network results can be derived from minimal or more targeted computation. Our solution 
proposes early-exit neural networks that present results as soon as the model is confident enough 
in its computation, saving energy while maintaining accuracy. Our project will apply this 
solution to seizure data and research to enable healthcare predictions.  
b) The basic goal of the project (what do you want to achieve at the end of the semester) 
Seizure Detection from EEG signals using wearable devices requires real-time processing that 
consumes minimal energy while maximizing accuracy. Traditional neural networks use the same 
computational cost, regardless of input complexity. Early exit architectures try to solve this issue by 
letting the network exit early if the network’s confidence is over a threshold. Existing approaches 
maintain constant width or increasing layer widths.  
Our project builds on these approaches by researching decreasing-width and adaptive-width 
early-exit architectures. Our hypothesis is that early layers need to capture and analyze more features in 
the input, which is the EEG patterns across channels and frequency bands in this case, while deeper 
layers focus on the specific discriminative features. This funnel type of design could reduce both 
memory usage and energy consumption compared to existing approaches to early exit architectures. 
c) Your assumptions and methods 
​
Our project is based on several assumptions. The core assumption being that not all EEG inputs 
require the same energy and depth of computation and that seizure patterns can be accurately identified 
in earlier layers. Secondly, we assume that architecture shapes, such as constant-width, increasing-width, 
decreasing-width, or adaptive-width layer structures, affect how efficiently and accurately inputs are 
detected. 
We tested these assumptions across 5 different model architectures: 
1.​ Deep Neural Network: Standard CNN without early exit, used as a baseline 
2.​ Constant-width early exit: Constant Layer width with exit heads at multiple depths 
3.​ Increasing-width early exit: Progressively wider layers with exit heads at multiple depths 
4.​ Decreasing-width early exit: Progressively narrower layers with exit heads at multiple depths 
5.​ Adaptive-width early exit: The network learns to remove irrelevant features dynamically. 
Our models are evaluated on accuracy, F1-score, exit-distribution, recall score, energy saved (FLOPs per 
inference), and confidence calibration.

f) Your current status and partial results; 
We have successfully made all the models, including the base CNN model, constant and increasing 
width architectures listed in an existing research paper, and decreasing and adaptive width architectures 
which is completely novel. The base CNN achieved 93.3% accuracy, 88.9% recall, and an F1 score of 
90.7% with no energy reduction. The constant-width model achieved 95.0% accuracy, 94.4% recall, and 
an F1 score of 93.5%, along with a 26.8% energy reduction. The increasing-width model performed 
poorly with 60.0% accuracy, 73.3% recall, and an F1 score of 59.6%, while only reducing energy by 
9.9%. The decreasing-width model matched the base CNN with 93.3% accuracy, 88.9% recall, and a 
90.7% F1 score, but achieved an 11.1% energy reduction. Finally, the adaptive-width model also had 
93.3% accuracy, 88.9% recall, and a 90.7% F1 score, while providing the highest energy reduction at 
38.2%. We have not properly tuned the models, and we’re still finding better solutions to figure out the 
thresholds at the exits, and we’re also researching other feature pruning mechanisms for the 
adaptive-width architecture.
